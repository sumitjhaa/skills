# 🎲 Decisions — The Neuroscience of Choosing Well

## What a Decision Actually Is

**A decision is a prediction.** Your brain simulates multiple futures and picks the one with the best predicted outcome.

**Problem:** Your brain's prediction system evolved for ancestral environments. It uses heuristics that were accurate then but misfire now.

**The cost of bad decisions:**
- Wrong career path → years lost
- Wrong job acceptance → misery
- Staying too long → missed opportunities
- Leaving too early → lost investment

---

## The Two Decision Systems (Kahneman's Model)

| System | Name | Speed | Energy | Accuracy | When to Use |
|--------|------|-------|--------|----------|-------------|
| **System 1** | Intuitive | Fast (milliseconds) | Low | High for familiar patterns, low for novel | Routine, low-stakes, familiar domain |
| **System 2** | Analytical | Slow (seconds-minutes) | High | High for complex, low for simple | Important, novel, high-stakes |

**The problem:** System 1 runs 95% of your decisions. It's wrong often but you don't notice because it feels right.

**The solution:** Learn when System 1 is reliable and when to deliberately engage System 2.

## Cognitive Biases That Fuck Up Your Decisions

### 1. Status Quo Bias

**What it is:** Preference for current state over change. "Better the devil you know."

**Neuroscience:** Loss aversion is 2x stronger than reward seeking. The amygdala fires more for potential losses than potential gains.

**How it fucks you:**
- Staying in a bad job because change is scary
- Not switching careers because you've invested time
- Not applying because rejection hurts more than missing out

**Counter:**
- **The 10/10/10 rule:** How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
- **Opportunity cost framing:** "By staying here, what am I giving up?" Not "What am I losing by leaving?"
- **The regret test:** "Will I regret NOT doing this in 5 years?"

---

### 2. Sunk Cost Fallacy

**What it is:** Continuing something because you've already invested resources, even when it's no longer worthwhile.

**Neuroscience:** Loss activation in anterior insula and ACC. Letting go of a sunk cost physically hurts.

**How it fucks you:**
- Staying in a degree you hate because you're 2 years in
- Continuing a project that's failing because you spent months on it
- Not quitting a job because you've been there 3 years

**Counter:**
- **Ask:** "If I had zero investment in this, would I start now?" If no, stop.
- **Ignore the past.** Past investment is gone. Only future outcomes matter.
- **The consultant test:** "What would I advise a friend in this exact situation?"

---

### 3. Confirmation Bias

**What it is:** Seeking information that confirms what you already believe and ignoring contradictory evidence.

**Neuroscience:** Dopamine fires MORE for information that confirms existing beliefs (the brain rewards itself for being "right"). Contradictory information triggers the error-detection system (ACC) — it feels BAD.

**How it fucks you:**
- Only reading positive stories about your chosen career path
- Ignoring red flags in a job offer because you want it to work
- Believing you're unqualified → only noticing evidence that supports it

**Counter:**
- **Actively seek disconfirming evidence:** "What would prove I'm wrong?"
- **The devil's advocate:** Argue the OPPOSITE position for 5 minutes
- **Blind spot protocol:** "What bias is most likely affecting me right now?"

---

### 4. Hindsight Bias

**What it is:** After an event, believing you knew it all along.

**Neuroscience:** The brain retroactively updates memories to fit outcomes. You literally remember being more certain than you were.

**How it fucks you:**
- "I knew that startup wouldn't work" (you didn't — you're rewriting history)
- "I should have seen this coming" (no you shouldn't have — the signals were ambiguous)
- Not learning the right lessons because you think you were "obviously wrong"

**Counter:**
- **Write down predictions BEFORE outcomes** (decision journal)
- **Reconstruct your pre-outcome state:** "What did I actually know at the time?"
- **Separate process from outcome:** A good decision can have a bad outcome. Judge the decision, not the result.

---

### 5. Availability Heuristic

**What it is:** Judging the likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind.

**Neuroscience:** The brain uses "ease of recall" as a proxy for "frequency." Vivid, recent, emotional events are easier to recall → they feel more likely.

**How it fucks you:**
- "Everyone I know is getting laid off" → you overestimate job market risk
- "That one person failed at my age" → you overestimate your chance of failure
- "I heard about someone who made it big in crypto" → you overestimate your chances

**Counter:**
- **Get base rates:** What % of people in your situation actually succeed? Not the story. The data.
- **Ask:** "Is this one vivid example, or is it actually common?"
- **Broaden your sample:** Don't rely on your immediate circle. Look at national data.

---

### 6. Overconfidence Effect

**What it is:** Overestimating your knowledge, abilities, or prediction accuracy.

**Neuroscience:** Dopamine from confidence obscures uncertainty signals. The more confident you feel, the less you detect errors.

**How it fucks you:**
- "I can learn this in a month" → takes 6
- "I don't need to prepare for this interview" → bomb it
- "I know enough about this topic" → you don't

**Counter:**
- **The confidence calibration:** "How confident am I? 90%? OK, let me list 3 reasons I might be wrong."
- **The pre-mortem:** "It's 1 year from now and this decision failed completely. What went wrong?"
- **Add 50% to your time estimates.** Add 100% if you're new to the domain.

---

### 7. The Framing Effect

**What it is:** The same option presented differently leads to different choices.

**The classic study (Tversky & Kahneman):**
- "90% survival rate" → 82% of doctors recommend surgery
- "10% mortality rate" → 54% of doctors recommend surgery
- SAME DATA. Different frame = different decision.

**How it fucks you:**
- "This job pays $70k" vs "I'm losing $30k by not taking the $100k job"
- "I have a 10% chance of success" vs "I have a 90% chance of failure"
- Same math. Different feeling. Different decision.

**Counter:**
- **Reframe both ways before deciding:** "What does this look like as a gain? As a loss?"
- **Ask:** "Would I decide differently if the frame were reversed?"
- **Use neutral language:** Strip the emotional framing. What are the raw numbers?

## Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

### The Uncertainty Types

| Type | Definition | Example | Strategy |
|------|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Known knowns** | You know what will happen | Salary if you take the job | Calculate |
| **Known unknowns** | You know what could happen, just not which | Company might grow or fail | Probabilistic thinking |
| **Unknown unknowns** | You don't know what you don't know | Industry disruption | Hedging, optionality |

**Most career decisions are "known unknowns" — you can estimate probabilities but can't be certain.**

---

### Probabilistic Thinking (For Known Unknowns)

Instead of "Will I get this job?" ask: "What's the probability I get this job?"

- "Will I get this job?" → binary (yes/no) → anxiety
- "What's the probability?" → 0-100% → reduces anxiety, enables strategy

**How to estimate:**
1. What % of people with my qualifications get this type of role?
2. What factors increase my probability? (network, prep, timing)
3. What factors decrease it? (competition, missing skills)
4. What's my best estimate?

Then: "If probability is 30%, I need to apply to ~10 similar roles to have a 97% chance of at least one offer."

**The math:** If p(success per attempt) = 30%, then p(at least one success in N attempts) = 1 - (0.7)^N

| N | At least one success |
|---|---------------------|
| 1 | 30% |
| 3 | 66% |
| 5 | 83% |
| 10 | 97% |

**This is why volume matters in job applications.** Not because each individual application has a high chance. Because enough volume makes success nearly certain.

---

### The Regret Minimization Framework (Jeff Bezos)

When he decided to start Amazon:
1. Project forward to age 80
2. "Will I regret NOT doing this?"
3. Yes → do it. The pain of regret > the pain of failure.

**The meta-lesson:** Regret is the only thing that's guaranteed. Failure is not guaranteed. Rejection is not guaranteed. But regret IS guaranteed if you don't try.

---

### Decision Speed: When to Decide Fast vs Slow

| Situation | Speed | Reason |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Reversible decision | Fast | You can change later. Speed beats perfection. |
| Irreversible decision | Slow | You can't undo it. Gather more data. |
| Low stakes | Fast | The cost of delay > cost of wrong choice |
| High stakes | Slow | The cost of wrong choice > cost of delay |
| Familiar pattern | Fast | System 1 is reliable here |
| Novel pattern | Slow | System 1 is unreliable here |

**Most career decisions are more reversible than they feel.** You can change jobs. You can change careers. It's harder than staying but possible. Don't treat reversible decisions as irreversible.

## The Decision Protocol

### For Important Decisions (System 2 Protocol)

```
Step 1: Define the decision clearly
  "I am deciding whether to ____"

Step 2: Identify your biases
  Which biases might be active? (status quo? sunk cost? overconfidence? loss aversion?)
  "One bias that might be affecting me is ____"

Step 3: Gather disconfirming evidence
  "What would convince me the opposite choice is correct?"

Step 4: Run the pre-mortem
  "It's 1 year later and this decision failed. What went wrong?"

Step 5: Test with the 10/10/10 rule
  "How will I feel in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?"

Step 6: Write it down
  Before you know the outcome. Record your reasoning. This kills hindsight bias.

Step 7: Commit
  Analysis without action is procrastination. Set a deadline for the decision.
```

---

## The 45-Second Decision Reset

When you're stuck in indecision:

```
0-15 sec:  "I am stuck in analysis paralysis. 
            This is my brain trying to achieve certainty in an uncertain world."

15-30 sec: "What is the minimum information I need to make a good enough decision?"
           (The perfect decision is the enemy of any decision)

30-45 sec: "I will decide based on what I know NOW. 
            I can adjust later."
           Make the call. Move on.
```

## The Evolutionary Summary

> **Your decision-making brain evolved for a world of immediate, concrete, survival-relevant choices with quick feedback. It did not evolve for a world of delayed, abstract, high-stakes choices with feedback that takes months or years. Every cognitive bias that fucks up your decisions was an ADAPTATION in the ancestral environment — loss aversion kept you alive, status quo preserved safe patterns, confirmation bias maintained social harmony. These were features then. They're bugs now. The solution is not to eliminate them (you can't). The solution is to recognize when they're active and deliberately override them with analytical thinking, probabilistic reasoning, and explicit bias-checking before every important choice.**

| Ancestral Decision | Modern Decision | Bias That Worked Then | Why It Fails Now |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|------------------|
| "Is that a predator?" | "Should I switch careers?" | Loss aversion (run first, ask later) | Career decisions need calculation, not fear |
| "Should I leave the tribe?" | "Should I leave this job?" | Status quo bias (stay with known group) | The tribe was the only option. Jobs are not. |
| "Is this food safe?" | "Is this job safe?" | Risk aversion (don't try new things) | Most new jobs will not kill you |
| "Should I trust this person?" | "Should I accept this offer?" | Confirmation bias (trust familiar signals) | Interview charm ≠ good workplace |
| "Is the herd moving?" | "Should I follow this trend?" | Herd mentality (safety in numbers) | Trends are often traps. Independent thinking wins. |
