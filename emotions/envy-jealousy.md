## Envy

### Evolutionary Origin
Envy is the **social comparison** emotion. It evolved to motivate you to compete for limited resources. If someone has something you need (food, mate, status), envy drives you to get it too.

Envy is not a sin. It's a **motivation system** that tracks your position relative to others.

### Neurochemistry
| Chemical | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Dopamine ↓ (relative deprivation) | "They have what I want, I don't" |
| Cortisol ↑ | Stress from social comparison |
| Testosterone ↑ (in competition context) | Readiness to compete |
| Serotonin ↓ | Reduced well-being from comparison |
| Norepinephrine ↑ | Alertness to opportunity |

### Brain Areas
- **Anterior cingulate** — detecting inequity ("this isn't fair")
- **Striatum** — reward comparison (their reward vs my reward)
- **Ventromedial PFC** — value comparison
- **Insula** — visceral discomfort of comparison

### The Two Types of Envy (Crucial Distinction)

| Type | Focus | Emotion | Action | Outcome |
|------|-------|---------|--------|---------|
| **Benign envy** | "I want what you have" | Admiration + motivation | Work harder | Growth, learning |
| **Malicious envy** | "I want you to lose what you have" | Hostility + resentment | Tear down | Stagnation, bitterness |

**Same trigger, different path.** The difference is whether you turn the energy toward yourself (improvement) or toward the other (destruction).

### Adaptive Function
Benign envy drives:
- **Motivation** — seeing others succeed shows what's possible
- **Learning** — who has what I want? What can I learn from them?
- **Ambition** — expansion of what you believe is possible

Malicious envy was adaptive in ZERO-SUM environments (ancestral contexts where resources were truly limited). If there's only one water hole and someone else has it, bringing them down is rational.

### Modern Mismatch
Most modern resources are NOT zero-sum:
- Someone else getting a job doesn't mean you can't
- Someone else succeeding doesn't reduce your potential
- Someone else having money doesn't make you poorer

But your brain treats ALL comparisons as zero-sum. This is the error.

### Protocol

**For benign envy:**
1. **Acknowledge it.** "I envy that. That means I want it."
2. **Learn from it.** What did they do to get there? What can you copy?
3. **Use it as a compass.** Envy points to what you actually want. Follow it.

**For malicious envy:**
1. **Name it.** "I'm feeling malicious envy. This is my scarcity brain activating."
2. **Check assumptions.** Is this actually zero-sum?
3. **Shift to curiosity.** Instead of resentment, ask: "How did they do that?"
4. **Scarcity to abundance reframe.** "Their success does not limit mine."

## Jealousy

### What It Is
Jealousy is the **fear of losing** something you have to a rival. Envy = wanting what you don't have. Jealousy = protecting what you do have.

### Neurochemistry
- Cortisol ↑↑ (threat to bond)
- Testosterone ↑ (male jealousy — competitor defense)
- Oxytocin ↓ (bond threat)
- Noradrenaline ↑ (hypervigilance to rival cues)
- Dopamine ↓ (loss anxiety)
- Serotonin ↓ (obsessive thinking about rival)

### Brain Areas
- **Amygdala** — threat detection (rival as threat)
- **Anterior cingulate** — social pain (fear of loss)
- **Orbitofrontal cortex** — obsessive rumination
- **Hypothalamus** — aggression readiness

### Adaptive Function
Jealousy evolved to **protect pair bonds**. A mate who doesn't guard against rivals is more likely to lose their partner and their reproductive investment. Jealousy = mate guarding.

### The Sex Differences (Evolutionary)
- **Male jealousy** — more triggered by sexual infidelity (paternity uncertainty)
- **Female jealousy** — more triggered by emotional infidelity (resource loss)

These are statistical tendencies, not absolutes. Both sexes feel both types.

### Jealousy vs Envy

| | Envy | Jealousy |
|---|------|----------|
| Situation | They have what I want | Someone might take what I have |
| Core | "I want that" | "Don't take that" |
| Focus | Their gain | My loss |
| Feeling | "I wish I had what you have" | "I'm afraid of losing you" |

### Protocol
1. **Distinguish real threat from imagined.** "Is my partner actually doing something wrong, or is my jealousy circuit misfiring?"
2. **Attachment insecurity work.** Chronic jealousy is about your attachment style, not your partner's behavior.
3. **Communicate, don't accuse.** "I feel jealous when X happens. Can we talk about it?" vs "You're making me jealous!"
4. **Self-soothe.** Jealousy is YOUR emotion. Your partner can help, but they can't fix it.

## Regret

### What It Is
The emotion that arises when you recognize that a past decision led to a worse outcome than an alternative. Regret requires **counterfactual thinking** — imagining what could have been.

### Neurochemistry
- Dopamine ↓ (missed reward)
- Cortisol ↑ (replaying the loss)
- Noradrenaline ↑ (mental replay)
- Serotonin ↓ (mood drop)

### Brain Areas
- **Orbitofrontal cortex** — comparing actual vs counterfactual
- **Anterior cingulate** — error detection
- **Hippocampus** — recalling the event and the alternative
- **Striatum** — reward prediction error (I could have had that)

### Adaptive Function
Regret is the **learning signal** for decision-making. Without regret, you would repeat the same mistakes. Regret = "I predicted that action would lead to X, but it led to Y. Update the prediction model."

### The Two Types of Regret

| Type | Source | Feeling | Use |
|------|--------|---------|-----|
| **Action regret** | "I did something I shouldn't have" | Specific, focused | Future behavior change |
| **Inaction regret** | "I didn't do something I should have" | Diffuse, lingering (haunts longer) | Future courage |

**Regret over inaction lasts longer** than regret over action (Gilovich & Medvec, 1995). Action regret fades. Inaction regret persists. This is why "I should have tried" hurts more 10 years later than "I tried and failed."

### The Regret-Rumination Loop
```
Decision → Bad outcome → Regret → Rumination ("what if") → More regret → More rumination
```

### Protocol
1. **Extract the lesson.** "What would I do differently?" One sentence. Write it down.
2. **Hindsight is not foresight.** You know now what you couldn't know then. You made the best decision with the information you had at the time.
3. **Use the regret for future decisions.** The regret signal DECREASES the next time you face a similar choice. That's the learning.
4. **If it's inaction regret:** Use it to take action NOW. Don't add to the pile.

## Nostalgia

### What It Is
A **bittersweet** longing for the past. Nostalgia mixes joy (the memory was good) and sadness (it's gone). It's not stuckness — it's emotional integration.

### Neurochemistry
- Dopamine ↑ (re-living positive memories)
- Serotonin ↑ (comfort, warmth)
- Oxytocin ↑ (social bonds — nostalgia often involves people)
- Cortisol ↓ (nostalgia is calming)

### Brain Areas
- **Hippocampus** — memory retrieval
- **Ventromedial PFC** — positive value of the memory
- **Insula** — emotional feeling of the past
- **Default mode network** — self-narrative, identity

### Adaptive Function
Nostalgia evolved to:
1. **Strengthen social bonds** — remembering shared experiences increases connection
2. **Provide existential meaning** — "I have lived a life with good moments" counteracts mortality anxiety
3. **Maintain identity coherence** — "I am the same person who experienced that good thing"

### When Nostalgia Helps vs Hurts

| Helps When | Hurts When |
|------------|------------|
| You feel connected to your past | You idealize the past and devalue the present |
| It reminds you of what matters | It prevents you from creating new experiences |
| It provides comfort during difficulty | It becomes a refuge from present problems |

### Protocol
- **Enjoy nostalgia, don't live there.** The past was good. The present can be too.
- **Use nostalgia as a compass.** What did that past joy have that you're missing now? Recreate it.
- **Shared nostalgia.** Nostalgia with others is bonding. Lonely nostalgia is isolating.

### Key Point
Resentment is **replayed anger**. Each replay triggers the original anger response. The person who wronged you has moved on. You haven't. You're the only one still paying the cortisol price.

## Boredom

### What It Is
The signal that your current environment offers **insufficient stimulation**. Boredom is not laziness. It's a motivation to seek better opportunities.

### Neurochemistry
- Dopamine LOW (no reward in current activity)
- Noradrenaline LOW (no alertness)
- Cortisol MODERATE (distress from under-stimulation)
- Default mode network ACTIVE (mind-wandering seeking stimulation)

### Brain Areas
- **Default mode network** — active when nothing is engaging (seeking mental content)
- **Salience network** — searching for something relevant to engage with
- **Striatum** — low dopamine = no perceived value in current activity

### Adaptive Function
Boredom drove ancestral humans to:
- Explore new territories (boredom of same campsite)
- Invent new tools (boredom of same methods)
- Seek new social connections (boredom of same faces)
- Create art, music, stories (boredom of silent nights)

Boredom is a **restlessness that drives progress**.

### The Two Types of Boredom

| Type | Source | Response | Healthy? |
|------|--------|----------|----------|
| Reactive boredom | "This specific thing is dull" | Switch activities | Yes — normal signal |
| Existential boredom | "Nothing feels meaningful" | Need meaning, not stimulation | Warning sign — check for depression |

### Modern Mismatch
The modern response to boredom is **passive consumption** (scrolling, watching). This is a DOPAMINE PATCH that treats the symptom without addressing the cause. It reduces the discomfort of boredom without the benefit of boredom (seeking something meaningful).

### Protocol
1. **Don't patch boredom with passive consumption.** The phone gives dopamine without solving the boredom's message.
2. **Ask the boredom question:** "What am I really in the mood to do right now?" Not "What can I consume?"
3. **Boredom as a signal for change.** Chronic boredom at work → career change signal. Chronic boredom in relationship → reconnection signal.
4. **Boredom tolerance.** Practice being bored without reaching for a phone. Creativity emerges in the gap between stimulation and action.

When comparison is eating you alive:

```
0-15 sec:  3 breaths. "I am feeling envy. 
            This is my social comparison circuit activating."

15-30 sec: "Their success does not limit mine. 
            This is not zero-sum."

30-45 sec: "What can I learn from them? 
            What do I want that I can now pursue?"
```
